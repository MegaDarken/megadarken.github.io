What people want is balance but with themselves as the fulcrum, those who seek to shift that balance will do well to keep in mind the fulcrum takes the brunt.

I think it is understandable that most people think of themselves as "Normal", they are their only reference point for the most part. Putting yourself in someone else's shoes is a phrase that is thrown about a lot, I feel it can be treated quite lightly by those who receive the phrase. It is neither easy nor comfortable to fully understand what others are going through, to understand why someone did that like that, how they came to that conclusion, what dictates their goals. It is hard to put yourself in someone else's shoes, especially if you have big feet.

My point being that; I think many lack the perception of their own weirdness and/or lack of weirdness, usually both at the same time. It is a relative metric, as it view it, it is effectively impossible for their to be more than 2 people to not fit into the category of having both someone more weird and someone less weird than them. The exceptions being the most weird and least weird persons and those who manage to exactly match those people's weirdness exactly, which is either hard or impossible depending on how the measurement is done.

As an aside; I think the weirdest person in the world would be a highly contested title but could arguably be feasibly determined, while determining the least weird person in the world I think is considerably more difficult. If you defined un-weirdness as average you might make some headway but I think you would also be barking up the wrong tree, focusing too much on physical characteristics that don't often come up in relation to discussions of weirdness, and likely eliminating the actual least weird person unintentionally. You would also run into the problem that who is the least weird person is likely changing constantly, based on any slight protuberances in the culture, even something as minute as if anyone in the world changes a habit or their perspective on something.

More broadly on those extremes; I have noticed a lot opposition to any implication that they are anything other than their own current understandings of how how weird they are, and while I can understand an inaccurate model of reality being a cause for concern, I still find it notable how impacted someone can seem about such a subjective and nebulous parameter. How much weight people can place on being on the extreme ends really does indicate how much value people can put on such judgement.

Another way to put 'weird' is 'standing out', being notably un-notable can be an effective strategy in many contexts: the modern office, the military, being a herd animal.

> "The nail that sticks out gets hammer."

On the flip side; standing out is good if you want to get noticed: a lot of entertainment roles, making friends, attracting a mate. Notoriety is the result of being the exception, you are interesting, you get the attention. Whether the visibility is good is another matter, the benefits can make themselves obvious but it is very much a double edged sword, it comes with the pressure of expectations, a loss of your privacy. It makes sense why many prefer to avoid "fame".

> "The squeaky wheel gets the grease"

The dichotomy of the desire for the two ends does not escape me, but I also think that the desired to be viewed as on either end is inherently quite unusual. Most people seem to actually want to in the middle, just weird enough to be casually interesting without becoming a spectacle.

On my own end I am not the best at evaluating how I myself am perceived but I would like think I am slightly weirder than people I see who think they are somewhat weird but aren't really that weird at all. I would describe people who actually call themselves weird as more in the category of quirky really. I think actually weird people are much less likely to use the term weird, as if the term does not even come into consideration. I think there is a lot of people weirder than me, but also a lot of less weird than me, and that's okay.

However, really I want to see an increase in overt weirdness overall, and I want people to be okay with that, to appreciate and truly engage with casual eccentrism. If you see something weird, esoteric perhaps, but with clear passion, think about your instinctive reaction, how would you hope someone will react to something you are passionate about?
